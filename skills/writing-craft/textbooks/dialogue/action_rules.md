# Robert McKee Dialogue — Action Rules

Sources:
- `/mnt/f/创作/奇迹/novel_lab/extracted/Dialogue_The_Art_of_Verbal_Action_Robert_McKee_zh.txt`
- `/mnt/f/创作/奇迹/novel_lab/新增教材收束笔记_Screenplay_Dialogue_2026-04-22.md`

Purpose: turn McKee's Dialogue principles into executable novel_lab gates for drafting and revision, with emphasis on verbal action, subtext, exposition timing, voice, conflict form, and anti-info-dump prose control.

## 0. Required preflight for any key dialogue scene

Before drafting a key dialogue scene, fill this compact map:

```text
Scene job:
Scene target / obstacle / result:
Speaker A wants:
Speaker B wants:
Power relation:
Conflict type:
Said:
Unsaid:
Unsayable:
Action verbs per exchange:
Information reader must know now:
Information to delay:
NPC voice cards, if any:
Expected turn at end:
```

If this map cannot be filled, the scene is not ready for prose.

## 1. Verbal action gate

Rule:
- Dialogue is action, not information exchange.
- Every important line must do something: probe, hide, refuse, soothe, pressure, deflect, bargain, seduce, warn, shame, invite, command, confess, deny.
- Each exchange must push the scene to the next beat or shift the speaker closer/farther from a want.

Fail if:
- A line only answers a question.
- A line only tells the reader a setting fact.
- The scene would mean the same thing if the line were removed.

Repair:
- Add a speaker want.
- Convert the information into a tactic.
- Make the reply resist, misdirect, overcomply, under-answer, or change the cost.

## 2. Said / unsaid / unsayable gate

Rule:
- For emotional, sacred, political, secret, shameful, romantic, or dangerous subjects, write three layers before prose:
  - Said: what the character chooses to say aloud.
  - Unsaid: what the character knows or feels but withholds.
  - Unsayable: what fear, taboo, rank, guilt, grief, ritual, or the unconscious forbids.

Fail if:
- Characters state the whole truth cleanly.
- A sacred or grief topic is treated as casual exposition.
- The reader has nothing to infer.

Repair:
- Move the direct meaning under the surface.
- Replace direct explanation with pause, correction, title, gesture, circumlocution, silence, or a safer substitute topic.

## 3. Exposition timing gate

Rule:
- Give exposition only when the character must use it now and the reader must know it now.
- Give the minimum needed to keep curiosity and empathy alive.
- Delay everything that does not change the current decision, danger, procedure, or relationship.

Fail if:
- Dialogue explains history before pressure requires it.
- One speaker gives complete institutional/worldbuilding answers.
- The scene pauses so the author can lecture.

Repair:
- Reveal through procedure, correction, mistake, taboo, negotiation, failed assumption, object handling, or cost.
- Split the information across beats and scenes.
- Attach each fact to a scene consequence.

## 4. No Q&A info dump gate

Rule:
- Avoid the pattern: newcomer asks / informed NPC answers / worldbuilding delivered.
- If a question is asked, the answer must be shaped by the answerer's want, fear, rank, impatience, shame, loyalty, or agenda.

Fail if:
- The answer is complete, neutral, and convenient.
- The asker learns without paying a cost or making a mistake.
- The responder has no current pressure.

Repair patterns:
- Correction: the protagonist fills a form wrong; the clerk corrects only the dangerous part.
- Refusal: the NPC refuses to explain because protocol, rank, grief, or fear forbids it.
- Partial answer: the answer gives one actionable fact and withholds the rest.
- Counter-question: the answerer tests why the protagonist wants to know.
- Consequence: the fact is learned because someone violates or nearly violates a rule.

## 5. Voice gate

Rule:
- Character voice comes from diction, syntax, rhythm, worldview, knowledge boundaries, status, pressure, and desire.
- Do not rely on catchphrases.
- A line should not be transferable to another speaker without loss.

NPC minimum voice card:

```text
Job:
Age / social position:
Current pressure:
Fear / what they want to protect:
Attitude toward protagonist:
Knowledge boundary:
Politeness level:
Avoidance habit:
Speech habit:
This scene's action verb:
```

Fail if:
- Everyone sounds like the author.
- NPCs exist only to deliver information.
- Dialogue is uniformly cold, abstract, polite, or rational.

Repair:
- Add job pressure and social risk.
- Give each speaker different avoidance strategies and emotional leakage.
- Rewrite using the speaker's professional vocabulary and rank etiquette.

## 6. Conflict type gate

Choose the dialogue conflict type before writing.

1. Balanced conflict
   - Use when parties have comparable leverage.
   - Each side attacks and defends; each exchange changes local advantage.

2. Comic conflict
   - Use for light daily friction, mismatch, and group warmth.
   - Comedy may come from timing, misunderstanding, status reversal, or overcommitment.
   - Do not harvest laughs by trivializing death, sacred relics, or mourning.

3. Asymmetric conflict
   - Use when rank, age, institution, money, law, or expertise is unequal.
   - Strong side can pressure with silence, procedure, title, interruption, or understatement.
   - Weak side survives through probing, politeness, loopholes, bargaining, or controlled insolence.

4. Indirect conflict
   - Use for political scenes, hidden villains, spies, taboo, and tests.
   - Surface topic must be functional; true attack runs under it.
   - Meaning leaks through what is not said, wrong titles, over-polite wording, and delayed reactions.

5. Reflexive conflict
   - Use for inner monologue, self-argument, memory, temptation, shame, and first-person narration.
   - The character must be divided against another self, not simply thinking aloud.

6. Minimal / implied conflict
   - Use for grief, reverence, intimacy, sacred ceremony, exhaustion, or fear.
   - Reduce words; use pauses, gestures, titles, and unfinished sentences.

Fail if:
- The conflict type is unclear.
- The tone breaks the ethical weight of the subject.
- The scene uses loud argument where silence would carry more pressure, or silence where active resistance is needed.

## 7. Sacred / grief / relic gate

Rule:
- Sacred and mourning topics require an unsayable layer.
- In the current novel_lab context, 英灵遗物 / 归航仪式 / death-return language must be handled through ritual, titles, avoidance, and cost.

Required devices:
- corrected title or formal address;
- lowered voice, pause, cut-off sentence, or changed wording;
- body etiquette: hands, stance, distance, object handling;
- visible consequence for misuse or flippancy;
- someone in the scene who knows the norm and reacts.

Fail if:
- A character casually says the equivalent of “dead people come back” without social or ritual consequence.
- Sacred objects are treated as ordinary equipment in dialogue.
- Comic conflict undercuts grief ethics.

## 8. Dialogue-result gate

Rule:
- A dialogue scene must end with a turn.
- At least one of the following must change:
  - relationship distance;
  - power relation;
  - information distribution;
  - emotional temperature;
  - risk perception;
  - decision or next action;
  - question for next scene.

Fail if:
- Characters simply exchange known facts.
- They leave with the same relationship and goal state.
- The scene can be summarized as “they talked about X.”

Repair:
- Add a withheld fact.
- Add a decision forced by the conversation.
- Add a misread, new suspicion, social debt, or irreversible promise.

## 9. Cross-cutting prose gates

These rules apply beyond quoted dialogue.

### 9.1 Narration voice separation
- Third-person narration may have literary style, but it is not the same as a character's strategic voice.
- First-person narration is dialogue-like: the narrator is doing something to the reader.
- Interior monologue must have conflict or strategy, not static explanation.

Gate question:
- Who is speaking, to whom, and what are they trying to accomplish by saying it this way?

### 9.2 Anti-author-voice gate
- If multiple speakers can swap lines without damage, the passage fails.
- If the narrator explains every subtext, the passage fails.
- If prose becomes abstract summary of feelings instead of behavior under pressure, revise into action, gesture, procedure, or partial speech.

### 9.3 Exposition/prose integration gate
- Worldbuilding in prose must obey the same timing rule as dialogue.
- Tell only what the current action makes necessary.
- Prefer sensory object, procedure, social correction, and cost over encyclopedia paragraphs.

### 9.4 Revision order gate
Fix in this order:
1. Dramatic need.
2. Scene target / obstacle / result.
3. Conflict type and power relation.
4. Speaker wants and line action verbs.
5. Said / unsaid / unsayable.
6. Exposition now/delay split.
7. Voice diction and rhythm.
8. Sentence polish.

Never polish sentence style before the scene has action, conflict, subtext, and a turn.

## 10. Hard failure checklist

A dialogue/prose passage fails if any of these are true:

- Question-answer information dump.
- No action verb per important line.
- No clear want for important speakers.
- No said/unsaid/unsayable where taboo, grief, secrecy, or sacred material is involved.
- NPC has no job/current pressure/attitude.
- All speakers share the same author voice.
- Exposition appears before reader/character need.
- Dialogue ends without relationship, information, pressure, or decision change.
- Sacred or mourning objects are treated casually without social consequence.
- Inner monologue is static explanation rather than reflexive conflict.

## 11. Minimal pass checklist

A scene passes when:

- [ ] Each important speaker has a current want.
- [ ] Each important line has an action verb.
- [ ] Said / unsaid / unsayable are mapped where needed.
- [ ] Conflict type is selected and visible in the exchange shape.
- [ ] Exposition is minimal, timed, and embedded in action/procedure/conflict.
- [ ] NPCs with more than functional use have voice cards.
- [ ] Dialogue produces a concrete turn.
- [ ] Prose narration does not explain away all subtext.
